How they found each other
The gym
She walked in unsure of everything — which machine, which weight, whether she even belonged there. He noticed immediately. Not in a predatory way. In a she looks lost and I know this place way. He offered to help. She hesitated, then said yes. That hesitation was the beginning of everything.
Training together — the intimacy of it
There is something quietly devastating about someone teaching your body how to be stronger. He corrects her form — careful, respectful, never invasive. He counts her reps with her. He pushes her past the point she'd have quit alone, and she starts to realize she's capable of more than she thought. The gym becomes a metaphor neither of them says out loud.
The walks home — where the real story lives
1
The first time — practical
He walked her home because it was late and it made sense. She didn't read into it. He noticed which street, which turn, which door. He filed all of it.
2
The second time — ritual begins
He just fell into step beside her without asking. She didn't tell him not to. They talked about everything and nothing. The walk home started to feel like the best part of the day for both of them — and only one of them admitted that, silently, to himself.
3+
Multiple times — now it's theirs
It's become a thing. Their thing. She looks for him when she's leaving. He's always there. Neither of them have named what this is. The street between the gym and her door has become the most important stretch of pavement in both their lives.
The three red roses — the most important scene so far
Weeks ago, walking past a florist, she had said — almost to herself — that she loves red roses. Not to him, not as a hint. Just out loud, the way you say things you love when you see them. He said nothing. He remembered everything.

Then one day, on the walk home, he stops at that same florist — or another one, or a street vendor — and comes back with three. Not a bouquet, not a grand gesture. Three. Specific. Intentional. He hands them over like it's simple. Like it's obvious.

"You said you liked them."

That's it. No speech. And that is exactly why it breaks her.
The same moment — two heads
Noah's head
He's been thinking about doing this for weeks. He almost didn't. He did it like it was nothing because he didn't want her to feel pressured. He watches her face when she takes them and has to look away.
Savannah's head
She laughs a little. Says something like "you didn't have to." Holds them against her chest the whole rest of the walk. Goes home, puts them in water, stares at them. Doesn't sleep right. Doesn't know what to do with a person who actually listens.
"Three roses. Not twelve, not one. Three — because that's how many he could buy without it looking like a declaration. He was already in love, and he was trying so hard not to frighten her."
